Malaysia's Mortality Analysis

Exploring the Population Trends and Death Patterns Across Malaysia (2000–2023)

Which States in Malaysia have the Highest Deathrates?

From the choropleth map, it can be observed that generally, states with larger populations tend to show more consistent death rate patterns across regions.

States such as Selangor, Kuala Lumpur, and Penang tend to have a more moderate death rate compared to others.

This could be due to better healthcare access and higher living standards in these urbanized areas.

In contrast, Perak, Perlis, Kelantan, and Sabah often show higher death rates per 100,000 people.

This might be because of limited healthcare access, weaker infrastructure, or a larger aging population in those regions.

The other states in Malaysia, shows a more consistent death rate with not much fluctuation between the years.

Do Death Patterns Differ by Gender & Ethnicity?

From the visualisation, states such as Selangor, Johor, and Perak record the highest total number of deaths, which corresponds with their larger population sizes.

When comparing between sexes, males consistently have higher death counts than females across most states, suggesting potential lifestyle or occupational risk differences.

Among ethnic groups, Bumiputera Malay deaths form the largest share, followed by Chinese and Indian populations. This is a pattern that broadly reflects Malaysia’s demographic composition.

Smaller states such as Perlis, Labuan, and Putrajaya show noticeably lower death counts, aligning with their lower population bases.

Overall, the visualisation shows a difference in death patterns between gender & ethnic groups in Malaysia. So in a sense, death patterns do differ, but it is more influcenced by the population size rather than the ethnic or gender itself.

How has the States in Malaysia Grown over the years? (2000-2023)

From 2000 to 2023, Malaysia’s population shows an overall upward trend across most states, with notable differences in growth rate.

Selangor records the steepest increase, driven by its economic opportunities, higher standard of living & its strategic location while Johor and Penang also show steady growth which might be due to the industrial and commercial expansion.

States such as Kedah, Perak, Negeri Sembilan, and Pahang experience moderate growth, which indicate that developments might have started to begin there.

In contrast, Kelantan, Terengganu, and Perlis show minimal changes, suggesting slower urbanisation and little opportunities to grow economically.

Kuala Lumpur’s population growth appears to level off in recent years, possibly due to limited space in the area and recent migration to Selangor as it is more affordable overall.

Putrajaya shows a steady rise after the early 2000s, consistent with its development as the administrative capital of Malaysia.

Overall, the visualisation reveals Malaysia’s continuous population expansion, with faster growth concentrated in economically active and urbanised states.

Are Gender Ratios the same across the Ethnic Groups in Malaysia?

Across Malaysia, the gender distribution is fairly balanced, though males consistently make up a slightly larger portion of the population than females.

This pattern can be seen across all three major ethnic groups: Bumiputera Malay, Chinese, and Indian which have been showing a consistent trend over the years.

The Bumiputera Malay community forms the largest share of the population, followed by the Chinese and then the Indian group.

Overall, the visualisation reflects Malaysia’s stable demographic structure with only minor variations between genders and ethnicities.

Does Death Rate Correspond to Population Size?

From the visualisation, it can be seen that Perak and Perlis consistently record higher death rates across the years. Whereby Perak's death rate peaked at 8428 people in 2022 while Perlis peaked 7422 in 2018.

Other than Perlis and Perak, Sabah also shows up with one of the highest amount of death rate in 2020 throughout the year 2000-2023 with 7884 people for that year alone. This might be due to Covid-19 pandemic whereby Sabah was one of the worst hit states during that period.

This suggests that population size alone does not determine the death rate in each state.

States such as Selangor and Kuala Lumpur have larger populations but have lower death rates, which could be due to better access to healthcare facilities, higher living standards and stronger public health systems.

However, Perak and Perlis generally have smaller populations but higher death rates. This may be influenced by limited healthcare access and slower socioeconomic development. Rural areas that usually make up most of these states, often have a higher concentration of elderly residents, naturally leading to increased mortality rates.

Overall, the visualisation highlights that death rate is shaped by multiple factors which includes demographics, healthcare accessibility, and socioeconomic conditions rather than just the total population size of a state.
